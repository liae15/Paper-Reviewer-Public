## Narrative Synthesis

These three sources approach AI in economic decision-making from complementary angles: practical capability assessment (Korinek), empirical performance measurement (Fish et al.), and normative governance (Yang and Zhai). Read together, they map the current state of the field from what agents can do, to how well they do it, to how they should be managed.

Korinek and Fish et al. both provide empirical grounding but reach different conclusions about readiness. Korinek's trajectory analysis is optimistic - agents are improving rapidly and already useful for routine research sub-tasks. Fish et al. temper this optimism by showing that even leading models achieve only ~70% of optimal performance on economic benchmarks and exhibit prompt sensitivity that undermines reliability. The tension between these findings suggests that agent usefulness is task-dependent: strong on structured, decomposable work (Korinek's research workflows) but brittle on tasks requiring robust economic reasoning under varied framing (Fish et al.'s benchmarks).

Yang and Zhai's ten principles address the governance gap that both empirical papers leave open. Korinek acknowledges the need for human oversight but does not formalize it; Fish et al. document capability shortfalls but do not propose what to do about them. Yang and Zhai's framework - covering agent decision-making, social interaction, and economic participation - offers a structure for managing the risks both papers identify, though their principles remain untested.

A shared limitation across all three sources is the absence of real-world deployment data. Korinek uses case studies and trend extrapolation, Fish et al. use stylized benchmarks, and Yang and Zhai synthesize existing literature. None evaluate AI agents making consequential economic decisions in live environments, which remains the critical gap for this research area.
