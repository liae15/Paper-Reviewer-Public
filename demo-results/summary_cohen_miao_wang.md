## Content Summary

This paper addresses the problem of dynamic pricing with demand learning under fairness constraints. Cohen, Miao, and Wang examine how retailers can set prices for different customer groups while satisfying two types of fairness: (1) price fairness, ensuring similar prices across groups and stable prices over time, and (2) demand fairness, ensuring similar demand levels across groups despite different willingness to pay. The authors propose learning algorithms (FaPU for price fairness and a primal-dual algorithm for demand fairness) and prove near-optimal regret bounds. Experimental results demonstrate that FaPU outperforms benchmark algorithms, achieving lower regret especially at smaller time horizons. The paper reveals that price and demand fairness are generally incompatible and cannot be achieved simultaneously.

| **Method** | **Quantitative/theoretical.** (1) Mathematical optimization framework formulating revenue maximization under fairness constraints; (2) algorithm design: FaPU (Fairness and Pricing with UCB-style exploration) for price fairness, and primal-dual online learning algorithm for demand fairness; (3) regret analysis using concentration inequalities and bandit theory; (4) numerical simulation comparing proposed algorithms against benchmarks (CILS: Constrained Index-based Learning Strategy; ILS-D: Index Learning with Demand constraints). |
| **Evaluation Criteria** | **Primary metric:** Regret—cumulative revenue loss compared to optimal pricing policy with full demand information. **Theoretical criteria:** Asymptotic regret bounds as function of T (time horizon), N (number of groups), K (time-fairness parameter), σ₀ (time-fairness threshold), δ (group-fairness threshold). **Experimental criteria:** Relative regret (percentage loss) across varying T, δ, σ, K, N values. **Success threshold:** Near-optimal regret scaling (matching known lower bounds for bandit problems). |
| **Findings** | **Empirical evidence:** (1) FaPU achieves 0.043% relative regret at T=1000, improving to 0.010% at T=10,000, consistently outperforming CILS (~0.110% to 0.018%) and ILS-D (~0.045% to 0.012%); (2) regret increases as fairness becomes stricter (smaller δ or σ₀); (3) regret scales approximately linearly in N but remains small in absolute terms. **Analytical inference:** (1) Price fairness regret bound O(KN²/(σ₀T^{1/4})) under assumptions; (2) near-optimal regret σ(N√T) achievable for sufficiently large T; (3) dynamic regret matches known lower bounds O(√(V_T T)) for non-stationary demand with variation V_T; (4) algorithms provably satisfy fairness constraints while learning. **Speculative claim:** At current trajectory, similar methods could extend to more complex fairness notions (authors note simultaneous price-and-demand fairness as open problem). |
